reads of the week


What the Luddites Really Fought Against

Richard Conniff for Smithsonian Magazine, Mar 2011

Queued this one up pretty nicely above, didn't I? Most people use the term Luddite as a pejorative to describe someone who is against technology like some kind of Amish. But the history of the term reveals a different orientation to technology -- one where the Luddites are not against techonology for the sake of it, but are against technology that is leveraged as a means to undermine workers and society in the pursuit of capital. A loosely-tied group of textile mill workers from across England in the early-1800s, the Luddites focused on destroying machines owned by mill owners who were partaking in exploitative working practices in a string of protests for better working condition and wages. These workers were not fearful of machines taking their jobs since the machines had already been in use for quite a long time. As such, the workers were intimately familiar and highly skilled with the technology. In response to these protests the state got involved with deadly force, in one of the many employments of government power to protect capital over workers.


While this article is a bit outdated, I find the recount of the Luddites to be fascinating. I also find it intersting to consider that the author's solution to creating a healthier relationship to technology is to touch grass which, valid! But also, 14 years after this was written, it really isn't possible to disconnect just by turning off your phone and going for a walk. The surveilance state is stronger than ever, which to me is the most concerning thing about where we're at (palantir, if you're reading this I'm just kidding! i love how you are tracking my every move ♥). One thing that rings true from this article is that the enemy isn't as clear-cut as it was in the 1800s. There's really nothing we can actually try to collectively destroy that will accomplish anything. So even though it's annoying, I'm still going to deny cookies, turn down the facial recognition when going through security at the airport, and use Instagram on my browser instead of downloading the app. I know that I'm the only person I'm inconveniencing by doing these things (and the TSA workers who have to look at my face), but it gives me an ounce of feeling of control over my digital footprint, which is maybe all we can ask for these days. I'll take whatever crumbs I can get!



A Brief History of Passementerie

Gina-B Silkworks

While we're on the subject of textile mills, a new craft was revealed to me this week: passementerie, which is the art of creating tassels, edgings, cords, and other such trimmings for textiles. I found out about Paul Dickman's Instgram, who is some guy trying to revive a defunct passementerie mill in Chicago, and shares a ton of mesmerizing videos of the creation of these trimmings. I have never thought too much about the production of these items before, and the history of this craft as a trade is pretty interesting.



The Medical Case for Self-Driving Cars

Jonathan Slotkin for The New York Times, 2 Dec 2025

Enjoyed reading this op-ed on the imperatives of making the streets safer. As a walker first, biker second, & driver third, it's impossible to ignore how terrible people are at driving. It's mind-boggling to notice how many people are on their phones at the wheel, and are completely ignorant of the fact that they're driving a two-ton hunk of metal. I trust Waymos since being a safer driver than most people is a very low hurdle, but I am not ready to fully accept autonomous vehicles into my life yet for a few reasons.


First is that I'm one of the sickos mentioned in the article that do actually enjoy driving. I get so bored and a little bit sick in the car as a passenger, so driving gives me just enough death-anxiety to stay alert and focused on a task that makes the trip more bearable. Second is that while it is undeniable that self-driving vehicles are safer than human drivers, I don't think the data cited by the author of this opinion piece paints a full picture. It's not clear from the data which percentage of crashes involved human interaction from the Waymo's human operator, nor is it clear to see (or maybe even possible to track) how many possible Waymo-caused crashes have been avoided by the interference of the human operators. That's kind of a nit-picky take, and I don't mean to discount the fact that more self-driving cars on the roads makes people safer, and that's the direction we need to move in a society.


One other thing that I'm holding out for is that we won't realize the true safety of autonomous vehicles until there's enough of them on the roads to be able to communicate with each other. When there's non-brand-locked communication between autonomous vehicles whereby they can know where every other self-driver is in the area, I think that's when I'll fully buy into this.

ROSALÍA - LUX

Apple Music, Spotify, Youtube

Simply amazing. There's so much about this album that I enjoy. The blend of Western European-classical & pop really works, and her voice is of course incredible. I have mixed feelings about what she chooses to reference in her music (she has a propensity to the annoying version of hot-cheeto-walk-the-mile-girl vibes, not the goth or laid back stoner version), but I appreciate that this album is rife with reference to sanctified women through history (and Mike Tyson calling a reporter a faggot). Currently my favorite song from this album is track 11, La Yugular, specifically the third verse, where she's singing about how big things fit in small things and small things fill up big things:

This song ticks both of those boxes.

etymologies of the week

All etymologies are taken directly from Etymonline, unless otherwise noted. Read more about Etymonline and the definitions of abbreviations in these etymologies here.

  1. hack (v.) - to cut roughly, with chopping blows, c. 1200
    • From the stem of Old English tohaccian - "hack to pieces", which comes from West Germanic stem *hakkon - "to chop", from the Proto-Indo-European root *keg- - "hook, tooth"
  2. Luddite (n.), c. 1800
    • Go back and read that article I linked above, or re-read it if you already forgot
  3. pellagra (n.) - a chronic disease caused by dietary deficiency (formerly blamed on diseased grain), characterized by dry, red skin, c. 1770
    • From Italian, a hybrid from Latin pellis - "skin" and Greek agra - "a catching, seizure" which is related to agrein - "to take, seize"
  4. warlock (n.) - a man claiming to practice sorcery or witchcraft
    • Comes from Middle English war-lou, from Old English wærloga - "traitor, liar, enemy, devil", from wær - "faith, fidelity; a compact, agreement, covenant", from Proto-Germanic root *wera- - "solemn promise, vow"
    • Seemingly, this is originally meant to be an "oath-breaker", or "one in league with the devil" which was recorded around 1300.
  5. emotion (n.) - a moving, stirring, agitation, c. 1570s
    • From French émotion (16c.), from Old French emouvoir - "stir up", which comes from Latin emovere - "move out, remove, agitate", from an assimilated form of ex - "out" and movere - "to move"
    • A Middle English term for "feelings, emotions" was wits of the heart (14c.)
