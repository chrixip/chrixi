reads of the week


What the Luddites Really Fought Against

Richard Conniff for Smithsonian Magazine, Mar 2011

Queued this one up pretty nicely above, didn't I? Most people use the term Luddite as a pejorative to describe someone who is against technology like some kind of Amish. But the history of the term reveals a different orientation to technology -- one where the Luddites are not against techonology for the sake of it, but are against technology that is leveraged as a means to undermine workers and society in the pursuit of capital. A loosely-tied group of textile mill workers from across England in the early-1800s, the Luddites focused on destroying machines owned by mill owners who were partaking in exploitative working practices in a string of protests for better working condition and wages. These workers were not fearful of machines taking their jobs since the machines had already been in use for quite a long time and were intimately familiar and highly skilled with the technology. In response to these protests the state got involved with deadly force, in one of the many employments of government power to protect capital over workers.


While this article is outdated I find the recount of the Luddites to be fascinating. I also find it intersting to consider that the author's solution to creating a healthier relationship to technology is to touch grass which, valid! But also, 14 years after this was written, it really isn't possible to disconnect just by turning off your phone and going for a walk. The surveilance state is stronger than ever, which to me is the most concerning thing about where we're at (palantir, if you're reading this I'm just kidding! i love how you are tracking my every move ♥). One thing that rings true from this article is that the enemy isn't as clear-cut as it was in the 1800s. There's really nothing we can actually try to collectively destroy that will accomplish anything. So even though it's annoying, I'm still going to deny cookies, turn down the facial recognition when going through security at the airport, and use Instagram on my Safari app instead of downloading the app. I know that I'm the only person I'm inconveniencing by doing these things (and the TSA workers who have to look at my face), but it gives me an ounce of feeling of control over my digital footprint, which is maybe all we can ask for these days. I'll take whatever crumbs I can get!



A Brief History of Passementerie

Gina-B Silkworks

While we're on the subject of textile mills, a new craft was revealed to me this week: passementerie, which is the art of creating tassels, edgings, cords, and other such trimmings for textiles. I found out about Paul Dickman's Instgram, who is some guy trying to revive a defunct passementerie mill in Chicago, and shares a ton of mesmerizing videos of the creation of these trimmings. I have never thought too much about the production of these items before, and the history of this craft as a trade is pretty interesting.



The Medical Case for Self-Driving Cars

Jonathan Slotkin for The New York Times, 2 Dec 2025

Enjoyed reading this op-ed on the imperatives of making the streets safer. As a walker first, biker second, & driver third, it's impossible to ignore how terrible people are at driving. It's mind-boggling to notice how many people are on their phones at the wheel, and are completely ignorant of the fact that they're driving a two-ton hunk of metal. I would say that I trust Waymos since being a safer driver than a most people is a pretty hurdle, but I am not ready to fully accept autonomous vehicles into my life yet for a few reasons. First is that I'm one of the sickos mentioned in the article that do actually enjoy driving. I get so bored and a little bit sick in the car as a passenger, so driving gives me just enough death-anxiety to stay alert and focused on a task that makes the trip more bearable. Second is that while it is undeniable that self-driving vehicles are safer than human drivers, I don't think the data cited by the author of this opinion piece paints a full picture. It's not clear from the data which percentage of crashes involved human interaction from the operator from the Waymo, nor is it clear to see (or maybe even possible to track) how many crashes caused by Waymos may have been avoided by the interference of the human operators. That's kind of a nit-picky take, and I don't mean to discount the fact that more self-driving cars on the roads makes people safer, and that's the direction we need to move in a society. One other thing that I'm holding out for is that we won't realize the true safety of autonomous vehicles until there's enough of them on the roads to be able to communicate with each other. When there's non-brand-locked communication between autonomous vehicles whereby they can know where every other self-driver is in the area, I think that's when I'll fully buy into this.

what i'm listening to this week


ROSALÍA - LUX

Apple Music, Spotify, Youtube

Simply amazing. There's so much about this album that I enjoy. The blend of Western European-classical & pop really works, and her voice is of course incredible. I have mixed feelings about what she chooses to reference in her music (she has a propensity to hot-cheeto-walk-the-mile-girl vibes), but I appreciate that this album is rife with reference to sanctified women through history (and Mike Tyson calling a reporter a faggot). Currently my favorite song from this album is track 11, La Yugular, specifically the third verse, where she's singing about how big things fit in small things and small things fill up big things:

This verse has been rolling around in my head all week. It's not so much the lyrics themselves that speak to me (like girl why are you singing about a glass of milk?), but rhythmically and sonically this verse is transcendent. The way that she phrases this in 12/8 time tickles me pink. And the timpani accompaniment is also perfectly offset from the meter change that just feels so good to listen to. A few thing that make me really like a song is when I really have to think about the rhythms in order to hum along, and when the lyrics are transitively constructed. This song ticks both of those boxes.
